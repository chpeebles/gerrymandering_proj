What is gerrymandering?

In the context of U.S. Elections, gerrymandering is the process of redrawing election maps in order to benefit one political party (usually the one in power).


What is redistricting?

Redistricting is the process of redrawing the geographic district lines used to elect members of the U.S. House of Representatives (and other state lawmakers depending on the state). It is done every 10 years following the U.S. Census.

Why gerrymandering is done

As stated before, Gerrymandering is done to give one side a particular advantage in U.S. House Races. Unless there is found to be racial gerrymandering (the practice of skewing maps in favor of a certain race, which is illegal), gerrymandered maps stay in place for 10 years until the next round of redistricting.


Why redistricting is done

Redistricting is done every 10 years because the U.S. Census gives us an update on the total population. See because the House of Representatives is determined by population, states will get a certain amount of seats based on how big their population (i.e. bigger states get more seats and smaller states get less seats). When the new population totals are determined, states will either get more seats (like Texas), lose seats (like California), or stay the same (like Alabama). To account for these changes, states will redraw their geographic lines used for congressional districts. These lines will remain in place for the next decade until the next census (which makes them all the more important!).

How is redistricting done?

There are actually three main ways a party can create a gerrymander. The most notable methods are in the form of cracking, packing and stacking.


Cracking - Cracking is the practice of spreading out a certain group of voters along the lines so that their impact is minimized.

Packing - Packing is the practice of cramming a certain group of voters into one district so that their overall impact on the electorate is minimized.
